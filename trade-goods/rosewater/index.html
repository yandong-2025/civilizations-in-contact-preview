Summary

Rosewater is a preparation consisting of water and oil of roses. It was used to scent clothing and as a perfume.

The legacy site connects rosewater to Chinese accounts of tribute, maritime commerce, and knowledge of Arab and Indian Ocean worlds. Several Chinese sources mention an envoy from Zhancheng, or Champa, presenting bottles of rosewater to the Chinese emperor in 959 CE.

The Rosewater example is useful for the prototype because it joins together a trade good, named travellers, specific cities, source texts, and several overlapping themes: aromatics, tribute, maritime trade, and ritual practice.

Zhu fan zhi 諸蕃志

Rosewater from Ta-shi

“Zhao Rugua says rosewater was made from the dew of flowers in the country of the Ta-shi, identified in the legacy material as Arab lands.”

Friedrich Hirth and W. W. Rockhill, trans. and annot., Chau Ju-kua: His Work on the Chinese and Arab Trade in the Twelfth and Thirteenth Centuries, entitled Chu-fan-chi, Part II, chapter 10, pp. 203-204.

The legacy source also discusses rosewater as chiangwei-shui and notes comparisons with attar of roses.

Ma Huan, Ying-yai sheng-lan 瀛涯勝覽

The Day of Worship in Dhofar

“On the day of worship, trading stops before midday; people bathe, put on clean clothing, use rosewater or incense oil, and perfume clothing and limbs before going to the temples of worship.”

J. V. G. Mills, trans., Ma Huan, Ying-yai Sheng-lan: The Overall Survey of the Ocean's Shores [1433], chapter 16, "The Country of Tsu-fa-erh [Dhufar]," p. 152 and note 3.

Ma Huan, Ying-yai sheng-lan 瀛涯勝覽

The People of Aden Present Tribute Gifts

“After the imperial will was read, people with precious things could sell or exchange them; the goods included amber, rosewater, giraffes, lions, patterned animals, ostriches, and white birds.”

J. V. G. Mills, trans., Ma Huan, Ying-yai Sheng-lan: The Overall Survey of the Ocean's Shores [1433], chapter 17, "The Country of A-tan [Aden]," p. 155.

Ma Huan, Ying-yai sheng-lan 瀛涯勝覽

The Ka'ba in Makkah

“Ma Huan's description of the Hall says that its walls were formed of clay mixed with rosewater and ambergris, giving off a perpetual fragrance.”

J. V. G. Mills, trans., Ma Huan, Ying-yai Sheng-lan: The Overall Survey of the Ocean's Shores [1433], chapter 20, "The Country of the Heavenly Square [Mecca]," pp. 174-175, note 7.
